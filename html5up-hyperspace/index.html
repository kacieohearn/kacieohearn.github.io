Kacie O'Hearn, M.S.

Hey there! I am a Biostatistics Ph.D. candidate at the University of Iowa. Welcome to my website where I've highlighted a bit about myself and my professional and personal endeavors.


I earned my M.S. in Applied Statistics in 2025 from the University of Wisconsin- La Crosse. My portfolio of mini-thesis projects included a Bayesian vs Frequentist power simulation study, permutation test analyzing the spread of non-parametric three dimensional data, determining criteria for detecting fake Swiss bank notes, predicting whether internet users will make a purchase online based on tracking data, creating a model to predict cat adoption status in the Austin, TX Animal Shelter, and predicting future floods in Wisconsin over time and space using spatial statistics and time series methods. In 2024, I graduated cum laude from UWL with a B.S. in Applied Statistics with minors of Computer Science and Mathematics.

About Me

I'm a statistician who is passionate about bettering society via statistics. Previously an actuary with experience in the health and financial sectors, I bring knowledge of both corporate and academic realms to the table.


My passion for biostatistics stems from my own personal health journey. At the age of 12, I suffered a stress fracture in my back, leading to many doctor appointments throughout my childhood. Doing work that directly improves the lives of others is my ultimate goal.


I'm a true Midwesterner, having lived in four of the Midwest states throughout my life. Happy to currently live in Iowa with my cat!

Outside of work, I enjoy reading, running, lifting weights, playing tennis, and enjoying a good TV show. As a newfound Hawkeye, I'll surely be cheering on Hawks athletics as well!

Research Experience

An Efficient Median Estimator for Three-Dimensional Rotations.

My advisor, Dr. Melissa Bingham, and I developed a measure of center that is proven to be robust to outliers and much more efficient than other median estimators for three dimensional rotations. This median estimator was then applied to foot joint data of humans, chimpanzees, and apes to analyze the true center of the rotation of each foot joint.
